📰 Industry News Roundup

CMS Skin Substitute Overhaul: What It Means for Your Practice
Effective January 1, 2026, Medicare eliminated individual biologic codes for skin substitutes. All products are now reimbursed under a single flat “incident-to” supply rate of approximately $127/cm² per application, regardless of brand or product type. Office-based and mobile wound care providers face significant reimbursement reductions, while hospital outpatient departments are expected to gain referral volume. Notably, planned LCD restrictions for diabetic foot ulcers and venous leg ulcers were withdrawn on December 24, 2025 — so coverage criteria remain unchanged, but the economics have fundamentally shifted.

Integrated Care Bundles Cut Wound Healing Time Nearly in Half
A new retrospective analysis published in the International Wound Journal found that implementing standardized integrated care bundles (ICBs) reduced mean time to wound healing from 25.5 weeks to 12.7 weeks — a 50% improvement. Wound infection rates dropped by 84.3% (from 7.3% to 1.3%) and mean labor costs fell by more than 70% compared to non-standardized care. The findings reinforce that consistent, protocol-driven wound care delivers measurable clinical and financial outcomes, and strengthen the case for structured documentation and treatment planning across care settings.

💡 Clinical Tip: Recognizing and Managing Biofilm in Chronic Wounds

Research suggests that more than 80% of chronic wounds harbor biofilm — organized communities of bacteria protected by a self-produced matrix that resists antibiotics and standard wound care. Biofilm is invisible to the naked eye, which makes it easy to underestimate as a barrier to healing.

When to suspect biofilm: A wound failing to progress after two to four weeks of appropriate care is the primary signal. Other indicators include persistently dull or flat wound tissue, recurrent low-grade inflammation without classic infection signs, and wounds that appear clean but simply stall.

Evidence-based management steps:

  • Debride regularly and repeatedly — sharp debridement physically disrupts the biofilm matrix. Schedule repeat debridement every 1–2 weeks as a planned part of care, not a one-time event.
  • Use topical antimicrobials, not systemic antibiotics — silver, cadexomer iodine, and PHMB-based dressings are first-line for biofilm-suspected wounds. Systemic antibiotics do not penetrate biofilm effectively and contribute to antimicrobial resistance.
  • Consider surfactant-based wound cleansers pre-debridement — agents containing EDTA or poloxamer surfactants can loosen biofilm architecture before mechanical removal, improving overall efficacy.
  • Document meticulously at every visit — record wound bed tissue type, exudate character, and periwound condition to track biofilm reduction and support medical necessity under payer guidelines.

📖 Source: International Wound Infection Institute (IWII) Consensus Document, 2022 Update
